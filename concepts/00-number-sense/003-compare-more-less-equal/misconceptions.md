# Common Misconceptions: Compare More, Less, Equal

Comparison errors often stem from visual perception bias:

---

## 1. The "Spread Out" Trap (Density vs. Quantity)
* **The Mistake**: If 4 apples are spread far apart, and 6 apples are clustered tightly together, the child might say the spread-out 4 apples are "more".
* **The Conceptual Break**: Lacking conservation of quantity; the child estimates based on the physical space occupied rather than counting.
* **How to help**: Line up the objects in matching rows so the comparison is clear, then count each set together to prove that 6 is more than 4.

---

## 2. "Fewer" vs. "Less" Vocabulary Confusion
* **The Mistake**: The child struggles to understand the word "fewer" or "less", but immediately understands "more".
* **The Conceptual Break**: Linguistic difficulty. "More" is an active, intuitive concept for kids; "less" or "fewer" requires understanding subtraction and absence.
* **How to help**: Frame questions both ways: "Which has more?" followed by "So which has fewer?"
