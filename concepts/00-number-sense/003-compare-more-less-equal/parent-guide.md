# Parent Guide: Compare More, Less, Equal

## What this concept means
Comparing groups involves understanding the relationships of "greater than" (more), "less than" (fewer), and "equal to" (same). Before kids use symbols like `>`, `<`, they must compare objects visually by matching them one-to-one.

## Why it matters
Comparing sets helps children understand the ordering of numbers (e.g. 5 is larger than 3) and is key to addition, subtraction, and algebraic estimation.

## How to teach using everyday objects at home
- **Lego Towers**: Build one tower of 6 blue bricks and another tower of 4 red bricks. Place them side-by-side. Ask: "Which tower has more?" "Which has fewer?"
- **Match-up**: Place 5 spoons and 4 cups on a table. Draw lines or physically align each spoon to a cup. The spoon left over shows there are *more* spoons than cups.
- **Snack Time**: Give yourself 3 apple slices and the child 3 slices. Ask: "Do we have the same, or does someone have more?"

## Questions to ask
- "Which plate has more strawberries?"
- "How can we make these two piles equal?"
- "Is 8 more than 6?"
