# Educator Guide: Misconceptions in Counting On

When transitioning to counting-on addition, children often make common errors.

---

## 1. Counting the Starting Number
* **The Mistake**: When asked to add $4 + 3$, the child starts at 4 and counts 4 as the first step: *"4 (step 1), 5 (step 2), 6 (step 3)"*, landing on 6 instead of 7.
* **Cognitive Break**: The child is confusing the *positional index* (being at 4) with the *operational action* (taking a step forward). They count points rather than the intervals/hops between points.
* **Correction Strategy**:
  - Use physical movement. Draw a number line on the floor. Have the child stand on 4. 
  - Say: *"You are standing on 4. If you take 0 steps, you are at 4. Now, take 1 step forward (child hops to 5). That is 1 step! Now 2 steps (child hops to 6)..."*
  - Help them see that they only count when their feet actually move to the next space.

---

## 2. Inability to Count On from the Larger Number
* **The Mistake**: Faced with $2 + 8$, the child starts at 2 and counts forward 8 times (*"2... 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10"*). While mathematically correct, this is inefficient and prone to error.
* **Cognitive Break**: The child does not recognize the commutative property ($a + b = b + a$) as a shortcut to make addition easier.
* **Correction Strategy**:
  - Show them two paths. *"Let's start at 2 and take 8 hops. Phew, that's a lot of hops! Now let's start at 8 and take only 2 hops. Which one was faster? Yes! We always start at the bigger number to count on."*
